Gilding the Evidence

What do the objects you carry with you have to do with true crime stories?

What do you carry in your pockets, or your purse, or your backpack everyday? What are the essentials before you leave your house, like your keys, wallet, phone? Does it change depending on where you're going? When was the last time you recorded what those things were? The things carried with a person say a lot about who they are, what kinds of things they do, and how they fit into a society.

People rarely record these types of items because they're such a mundane part of their everyday lives. One of the few instances where they are written down is when someone is the victim or perpetrator of a crime, especially in true crime reporting, where writers seek to bring their stories to life with juicy details.

Because the Gilded Age, roughly 1870-1900, was known for its abundance of dramatic, sensational newspaper reporting, it serves as an ideal period for studying how people perceived these objects and the people who carried them, especially in the context of crime and victimhood. This period is also known for the shifting roles of immigrants, racial minorities, and women. Possessions are particularly important sources of information about these lives, as they tend to have fewer surviving written records than other groups.

Take a look at examples of these objects from museum collections to better understand how they reflected who these people were, and who other people thought they were. Then step into the shoes of someone that lived in the year 1885, and see how their identities and the choices they made affected how their daily activities played out.

The Gilded Age

The Gilded Age is named after the satirical novel The Gilded Age: A Tale of Today, co-written by Charles Dudley Warner and Mark Twain in 1873. The book drew attention to the corruption, excess, and individualism that they saw as defining their time, and historians in the early twentieth century retroactively applied their title to the last few decades of the nineteenth century. The term "gilded" referred to the excessive displays of wealth by the rich and famous which covered up deeper societal problems.

Historians debate exactly what time period should be considered the Gilded Age, some using the end of the Civil War in 1865 as a starting point and others using the end of Reconstruction in 1877. The Spanish-American War in 1898 is sometimes used to mark the end of the period, and sometimes it's the end of the century in 1900. Scholars often study the Gilded Age alongside the Progressive Era that followed it, which featured greater efforts to reform some of the corruption and inequality that plagued the Gilded Age.

The last few decades of the nineteenth century saw dramatic changes in how Americans lived. The United States experienced a rapid period of urbanization, with three quarters of the population living in rural areas in 1870, two thirds in 1900, and by 1920 more Americans lived in cities than outside of them for the first time. This was mostly concentrated in the industrialized Northeast where nearly two thirds of people lived in cities by 1900, compared to around 40% in the West and 18% in the South.

This period also featured an explosion of new technology that affected everything from agriculture and communication, to transportation and manufacturing. This led to an increase in the production of affordable newspapers and magazines, and made consumer products, including luxury goods, available to more people at all levels of society.

Of course, not everyone experienced the Gilded Age in the same way. What is referred to as the Gilded Age for America as a whole is sometimes called "The Tragic Era" by scholars of African American history, as progress made during Reconstruction started to unravel. Women, minorities, and immigrants in particular filled new roles in their communities in both positive and negative ways.

Personal Possessions

The Gilded Age marked a new frontier in manufacturing, selling, shopping for, and paying for things. For the first time, a certain level of luxury and goods were available to people at nearly all levels of the social ladder. Labor was cheaper due to the massive waves of new immigrants, goods were cheaper to make and distribute due to innovations in manufacturing, transportation, and communication, and the lack of military conflict allowed people to focus their money and attention in new materialistic directions.

People rarely record the things they carry with them because they're such a mundane part of their routine. These overlooked details usually only become worthy of writing down when something out of the ordinary happens to someone, which most often means becoming involved in a crime. Whether victim or perpetrator, or somewhere in between, sensationalist journalists of the Gilded Age used these details to add depth to their accounts of true crime, which allowed readers to draw conclusions about the people involved in the story.

Calling Cards

Tortoiseshell calling card case, c. 1840-1890. National Museum of American History.

Similar in style to a business card, calling cards started to be used in Western society in the eighteenth century. People would print their name and sometimes their address on a small card and could give it to a new acquaintance so that they could pay them a visit. One could also bring their cards when calling on a friend's home, either giving it to a servant to announce their visit or leaving it at the door if the friend wasn't home.

During the Gilded Age these were associated with both men and women, predominately white Americans, and reflected their roles as active and social community members.

The Game

If you were carrying a calling card, that meant your neighbors could expect to receive visits from you and you might receive visits from them in return. If they see you as polite, respectable, and social, your neighbors might be more willing to help you and do you favors.

For more information, see:

Mayor, A. Hyatt. “Old Calling Cards.” The Metropolitan Museum of Art Bulletin 2, no. 2 (1943): 93–98. https://doi.org/10.2307/3257136.

Memorandum Book and Pencil

Memorandum Book, 1870. National Museum of American History.

It started to become common in the late eighteenth century for printers and stationary shops to sell small pocket "memorandum books," essentially a small notebook, in which people could record personal thoughts and reminders, business ledgers, or take out a page to leave a note for someone else.

Many men, particularly white men, were recorded with these kinds of notebooks in their possession during this period. Indeed, these notebooks were often marketed to men specifically so that they could record important business notes on the go. As many people became more focused on business and industry, practical and portable objects like these became a symbol of their role in the public sphere.

The Game

The ability to leave notes lets a memorandum book double as a sort of informal calling card. You could leave your name and address with a shopkeeper, for instance, which they could use to deliver purchases to your home.

For more information, see:

Colclough, Stephen. “Pocket Books and Portable Writing: The Pocket Memorandum Book in Eighteenth-Century England and Wales.” The Yearbook of English Studies 45 (2015): 159–77. https://doi.org/10.5699/yearenglstud.45.2015.0159.

Handkerchiefs

Handkerchief, fourth quarter of the nineteenth century. Metropolitan Museum of Art.

Handkerchiefs have been a part of the American material world since the beginning of the Colonial Period. They were usually made of silk, cotton, or linen, and could be embroidered, printed on, or trimmed with lace. They served the same function as modern facial tissues, generally cleaning up small messes.

Handkerchiefs were carried by men, women and children at all social levels. They were usually inexpensive and sold by the dozen, and shirts often came with specific handkerchief pockets. They could be plain and simple, or dainty and ornate.

The Game

These can be a symbol of refinement, or a practical item for cleaning or picking things up. Whether people see you as elegant and dainty or if you're more nimble grabbing things, it might make it easier to take things you're not supposed to.

For more information, see:

Pistols and Revolvers

Smith and Wesson Revolver, 1881–1902. Metropolitan Museum of Art.

Though firearm ownership was deeply entrenched in American culture long before the Second Amendment was added to the Constitution, gun culture began to take on deeper roots in the social consciousness following the Civil War. The emergence of rifle clubs, militias, and cultural depictions of the West being "tamed" with the aid of sharp shooters made pistols and revolvers an important element of Gilded Age material culture.

Like with other violent weapons such as knives, firearms were most commonly associated with men in depictions of crime during the Gilded Age. Gun carriers in newspaper reports were also much more often white Americans than a racial minority or an immigrant.

The Game

If someone sees that you are carrying a firearm, they probably think that you are anticipating trouble or seeking it out. Depending on who you are, people will probably assume which one it is and either mistrust or target you.

For more information, see:

Cramer, Clayton E. Lock, Stock, and Barrel: the Origins of American Gun Culture. Santa Barbara: ABC-CLIO, 2018.

Knives

Folding Knife, c. 1885-1888. National Museum of American History.

People have been carrying sharpened blades since well before recorded history, with portable folding knives starting to become widely available in England during the Industrial Revolution. A knife can cover a wide range of shapes and purposes, from a tool used in manual labor to a weapon used to attack or defend.

Like with firearms, knives in newspaper accounts of crime were more often associated with men, as women were less often associated with violence in general.

The Game

Depending on the situation and who you are, having a knife could suggest to others that you're a hard worker with tools to get a job done, or that you're ready to grapple with violence. Like the pistol, it might make people mistrust you, or target you for a crime.

For more information, see:

Moore, Simon. Penknives and Other Folding Knives. Risborough, Buckinghamshire: Shire Publications, 2008.

Keys

Hunneman Fire Engine House Keys, 19th century. National Museum of American History.

Keys are both very practical and very symbolic objects. They grant access to a space, and represent trust and authority over a certain domain.

Both men and women were depicted with keys during the Gilded Age, though historically women were more commonly associated with keys, as the "keyholders" of the home and the domestic sphere in general. The term "chetalaine" during the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries was used to both refer to the mistresses of an important house, and to the elaborate and decorative belt hooks they wore that would hold keys and other small accessories.

The Game

Regardless of what walk of life you're from, you still need keys to get into your house or place of business.

For more information, see:

Cummins, Genevieve E., and Nerylla D. Taunton. Chatelaines: Utilities to Glorious Extravagance. Woodbridge: Antique Collectors Club, 1994.

Newspaper Clippings

Newspaper Clipping, 1888. National Museum of American History.

Though newspapers often print on cheap and easily disposable paper by design, allowing them to print more frequently and abundantly, people have always preserved pieces that were important to them. Sometimes they are articles personally related to them, like a wedding or birth announcement, or it's just a piece they would want to reread later. While some of these articles wind up in scrapbooks, others were carried loose or tucked into notebooks.

Both men and women would sometimes carry newspaper clippings with them. In newspaper depictions, these were usually well-to-do people of good social standing, usually white Americans or more respected immigrants like Germans. Someone who read and collected items from the newspaper likely had the leisure time to do so, and therefore probably was financially stable and somewhat educated.

The Game

Someone with a certain degree of leisure time and education would probably seem more respectable and trustworthy. That might encourage people to do them favors.

For more information, see:

Ritzenthaler, Mary Lynn. “Preserving Newspaper Clippings.” Prologue Magazine, Spring 2016.https://www.archives.gov/publications/prologue/2016/spring/preserve-clippings.html.

Rings

Varina Davis' Wedding Ring, c. 1850-1880. National Museum of American History.

Like with many other consumer goods, increased industrialization and efficiency in the jewelry industry allowed more people to be able to afford decorative pieces, partially contributing to the "gilding" that defined the culture of the period. Diamonds and silver became more popular with improvements in mining technology, and designs mirrored the Art Nouveau style that permeated visual culture throughout the Western world.

As one might expect, rings were most often associated with women. In newspaper articles, such decorative items were an important part of establishing the femininity of the subject and their association with beautiful, domestic comforts. White American women were depicted with these kinds of objects much more often than African American or immigrant women, implying something both about their higher economic status and about how society saw the femininity of marginalized women.

The Game

In any time period, flashy jewelry can make you a target for theives. Watch your back!

For more information, see:

Wees, Beth Carver. “Nineteenth-Century American Jewelry.” In Heilbrunn Timeline of Art History. New York: The Metropolitan Museum of Art, October 2004. http://www.metmuseum.org/toah/hd/ajew/hd_ajew.htm.

Pocket Watches

Pocket Watch, c. 1882. National Museum of American History.

With the explosion in factory jobs and faster transportation, the nineteenth century marked an important moment in the need for precise timekeeping. Though they were fairly expensive, pocket watches were both a luxury and a functional necessity for many people from workers to business barons.

Like memorandum books, watches were marketed more towards men as an accessory of a mobile capitalist lifestyle. Some women did carry them in a shirtwaist pocket or on a belt, often in smaller and more decorative designs.

The Game

Because watches could be expensive, and were easy to pawn or sell, they could be a common target for robbers and pickpockets.

Crime and Journalism

Cheap daily newspapers started to appear in droves during the Gilded Age partially due to the “Detroit plan” pioneered by Detroit Eventing News founder James Scripps, who cut production costs in 1873 by printing on smaller pages with fewer columns. New waves of European immigrants, readership from newly emancipated former slaves, and increased literacy among the population in general also encouraged more frequent printing and wider circulation. The increase in manufacturing and selling of consumer goods also fed this trend, as more things to sell and media to sell them in fueled the demand for advertising space, which in turn increased the output of sensational, highly readable articles.

True crime became one of the defining features of this type of journalism. Many of the great literary detectives—Sir Arthur Conan Doyle’s Sherlock Holmes, or Edgar Allan Poe’s Monsieur C. Auguste Dupin—were created in the latter half of the nineteenth century, setting the tone and formula for all forms of crime writing and reporting. These fictional criminal tales often took the style of a game, drawing in readers and presenting the situation as a low-stakes competition with thrilling secrets and twists. Readers of true crime were able to approach the story in the same way, using the gruesome details provided to try and piece together the case for themselves.

The high number of daily newspapers led to heavy competition between publications, which often drove journalism to become more dramatic and graphic. The relative peace following the end of the Civil War left a vacuum in reporting filled with mayhem and bloodshed, and to fill this demand journalists took to covering thrilling accounts taken from police sources and courtroom transcripts. Stories like these, by frequently including images, vivid details, and playing on emotions, required less analysis and mental exertion for readers than stories like politics and current events.

Immigration, Race, and Gender

The Gilded Age marked a period of significant changes, especially for new immigrants from Europe, African Americans, and women. The number of European-born people living in the United States jumped from almost five million to almost nine million between 1870 and 1900. Most flocked to urban, industrialized centers where they took low-wage manufacturing or manual labor jobs. People of different origins were perceived differently by American-born people. Groups like Italians and Irish were generally considered impoverished, violent, and suspiciously Catholic, while groups like Germans, who had been in America longer and were more likely to be skilled craftsmen, had better reputations.

By the 1870s slavery was less than a decade in the past, and the Constitution had recently gained its Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments granting new freedoms to African Americans. While several thousand Black men entered leadership positions in government during Reconstruction, and the Freedmen’s Bureau helped formerly enslaved people gain access to new opportunities, much of this progress was undone after Reconstruction ended in 1877, and by 1900 upwards of 90% of African Americans were doing much of the same agricultural work for many of the same employers who had once been enslavers.

Women were increasingly able to experience life outside the home, and the number of employed women jumped from nearly 1.8 million in 1870 to almost 5.2 million in 1900. However, like today, the female experience was not universal and intersected with other factors like race, class, and national origin. By 1900 around 43% of African American women, 19% of foreign-born women, and 25% of first-generation women over the age of sixteen were in the workforce, compared to 15% of white women with American-born parents. White American and European women were often portrayed in media as vulnerable, pretty, and domestic, while Black women were more likely to be shown without this attractive fragility, acting on their own in a distinctly "unfeminine" way.

Other immigrant groups and racial minorities did not appear as frequently in Gilded Age media, likely because they made up less than 1% of the population by 1900. However, in their infrequent appearances they seem to occupy a similar spectrum of acceptance to mistrust as European immigrants did, ranging from a sinister perception of Chinese workers to a more welcoming but mysterious view of wealthy Latin American merchants.
